Project 1 ‚Äî Friendly Campus for Atlantis Underwater Visitors

HKUST will host visiting mermaid students from Atlantis. We need to adapt our land campus to their body, habits, and culture. This page introduces the project and links to my diary and reflection.

Description & Goal

POV: A mermaid exchange student needs water‚Äëbased infrastructure and learning tools. Their anatomy and habits do not fit a dry, stair‚Äëfilled, screen‚Äëbased campus. They should be able to study well and live comfortably in HK. They should also be able to order food easily at cafeteria kiosks.

Needs & Obstacles

  • Mobility: Escalators, stairs, and long paths are hard for mermaids. They need water slides, swim tubes, and water elevators so they can move without leaving water.
  • Academic: Paper and normal electronics get wet and stop working. They need waterproof devices and materials so they can study and take notes.
  • Health: Dry air dehydrates them. They need easy access to seawater hydration stations and humid spaces. For long dry routes, wearable water enclosures could help them stay comfortable.
  • Social: Food and clubs are mostly designed for land students. They often eat fish and seaweed, so more aquatic cuisine and inclusive activities would help them join campus life.

Project Goal: Design campus policies, infrastructure, and tools so mermaid students can study, socialize, and live well. We will start with cafeteria kiosks: make the ordering flow easy with wet hands, using a waterproof stylus and bigger targets.

Learning & Execution Process

Needfinding

  • We wrote down the basic facts about our users. Mermaids have wet fingertips, a tail instead of legs, and need water often. They can breathe on land for some time and are very strong swimmers. This tells us where normal campus design may fail.
  • We listed problems in four areas: moving around campus, doing school work, staying healthy, and joining the community. This helps us see the full picture before we pick one area to fix.

Ideation

  • Infrastructure: We imagined ways to move in water across campus. Ideas include water‚Äëfilled tunnels, waterslides, swim tubes, water elevators, and hydration domes. These ideas remove the need to be dry and make travel easier.
  • Social & cultural: We proposed aquatic dining halls, mixed land‚Äësea sports, and events that both land students and mermaids can enjoy. This reduces social barriers and food problems.
  • Tools: We suggested waterproof laptops and tablets, small water scooters for long trips, and a waterproof stylus for kiosks. For this project, we focus on the kiosk flow for wet hands because it is small, testable, and useful.

  • Persona (mermaid student)

Use of AI

Used cursor to help create this website

Personal Contributions & Achievements

  • Led early brainstorming and created the initial mermaid persona.
  • Narrowed the problem scope and chose the school domain to focus on.
  • Took detailed notes during group work to capture decisions and evidence.
  • Facilitated group discussions to align on the POV and next steps.
  • Presented our findings and rationale in the final in-class presentation.
  • Applied accessibility thinking: wet hands, tail mobility, and hydration needs.
  • Used usability heuristics (visibility, error prevention, match to users) to judge ideas.
  • Mapped task flows for kiosk ordering to find pain points and quick wins.

Personal Reflection

Designing for mermaid students forced me to put accessibility first. A kiosk that works for dry hands and upright posture will fail when hands are wet and users have a tail. I learned to translate these physical constraints into interface choices: bigger touch targets, higher contrast, short steps, and clear recovery when water causes input errors.

  • Accessibility focus: Support wet‚Äëhand input (moisture‚Äërobust touch and a waterproof stylus), generous target sizes, readable typography, and strong contrast. Keep controls in easy‚Äëto‚Äëreach zones for users who cannot stand close or upright.
  • Universal design: Multimodal input (touch + stylus), captions for any audio, and plain language labels help both mermaid and land students.
  • Process mindset: Start with low‚Äëfi wireflows, test with short, observable tasks (place an order, edit an item, pay), and track time on task and error rate to guide iterations.

Overall, accessibility constraints did not limit creativity; they focused it. By designing for the hardest case (wet hands, non‚Äëstandard posture), the kiosk becomes simpler and better for everyone.

Project 2

Description & Goal

This project explores how students cope with food access during typhoons while living in HKUST dorms. Many students do not have full kitchens and cannot easily buy groceries when stores and canteens close. Our goal is to design a chatbot that helps students make quick meals with what they already have, reduces stress, and provides clear, supportive guidance during emergencies.

Part 1: Empathize

Observe / Interview

During Typhoon Ragasa, Emily (Hall 4 Resident) couldn't leave campus to buy groceries. Nearby stores were nearly empty, and Hall 4's shared kitchen created long queues."‚ÄúI only had a few cans of soup and instant noodles left. The store was out of vegetables and eggs, and our dorm only has a stove‚Ä¶ I had to wait a long time to use it.‚Äù

Our interview transcript captured Emily's struggles with grocery access during the typhoon, long kitchen queues, and limited ingredients. Our observations documented the differences between halls with full kitchens versus those with only microwaves, the suspension of delivery services, early canteen closures, and safety restrictions on cross-hall kitchen use.

Additional Observations

Halls without kitchens (e.g., Hall 9) often have only microwaves and water dispensers. Delivery suspended; canteens closed early; rationing created uncertainty. Borrowing kitchens across halls was restricted for safety.

Scenario: Typhoon Season in Student Dorms

A voice/chat agent could provide real-time updates on open canteens and stores. It could suggest simple recipes using current ingredients and appliances (e.g., microwave-only). It could coordinate shared kitchen use or food-sharing among hallmates. It could offer safety tips and supportive messaging to reduce anxiety.

Part 2: Ideate

Task 1 ‚Äî Point of View (POV)

From our interviews, we learned that many students struggle to eat properly during typhoons. Canteens may close, ingredients are limited, and most dorms only have basic kitchen tools. Students often end up eating instant noodles or skipping meals. They want fast, simple help and clear guidance on how to cook with what they already have.

We explored whether a chatbot could help by showing which canteens are still open, coordinating shared kitchen use, or suggesting easy microwave-based meals. Our goal was to create a chatbot that provides comfort, clear instructions, and practical recipes that work in dorm conditions.

POV Statement:
A student stuck on campus during a typhoon needs quick, appliance-aware meal suggestions and reliable food access information because kitchens are limited, stores may be closed, and stress is high.

Task 2 ‚Äî Brainstorming: Chatbot Ideas

  • Ingredient-to-Recipe Bot: Enter ingredients ‚Üí receive simple recipes matched to available appliances (text or voice guidance).
  • Cooking Guide Bot: Hands-free voice steps (‚Äúwhat‚Äôs next?‚Äù) for beginner-friendly cooking.
  • Food Availability Bot: Shows which canteens/stores are open with maps or walking time.
  • Shared Kitchen Bot: See stove/microwave availability and coordinate ingredient swaps.
  • Smart Shopping Bot: Sends pre-typhoon reminders with short grocery checklists.

Part 3: Prototype

Prototype Iterations

First Iteration

We began with a simple chatbot where users typed their available ingredients and received one or two quick recipe suggestions. This showed the core idea clearly but did not feel very personalized.

Second Iteration

We added more user preferences such as diet type, allergies, and ingredient restrictions. However, during testing, students said it felt too slow and overwhelming, especially during stressful typhoon conditions.

Third Iteration

We simplified setup to only the essential preferences (e.g., dietary type and appliance availability). Users could immediately start entering ingredients. This made the chatbot faster and calmer to use, and this version became our main prototype moving forward.

Part 4: User Testing

We tested our final chatbot, Typhood, using a Google Form. Students rated ease of use, usefulness, and the quality of recipe suggestions. We also collected open-ended feedback about what felt helpful and what needed improvement.

Initial Feedback

  • Users wanted a short guide explaining how to start using the chatbot.
  • The early setup asked too many questions and felt overwhelming.
  • A simple logo or visual identity would help the chatbot feel more memorable.
  • Speech input would make listing ingredients faster and easier.

After Iteration

  • The chatbot became easier and faster to use.
  • Users liked that Typhood adjusted recipes based on available appliances.
  • Both text and speech input worked well for casual ingredient input.
  • Some users suggested adding photos to help compare and choose meal options quickly.
  • Link to testing results

Use of AI

I used generative AI to help draft the prototypes and think of user testing questions. Final design decisions, feature prioritization, and prototype layout were made by our team based on interview feedback and user testing results.

Personal Contributions & Achievements

  • Led development of the prototype, features and demos
  • Designed and iterated on the UI across all three prototypes.
  • Ran multiple user testing sessions and synthesized feedback into clear design changes.
  • Presented the final prototype in the in-class presentation, along with creating the final presentation

Personal Reflection

I learned that even small barriers in setup or onboarding can discourage use during stressful situations. People want tools that feel calm, fast, and supportive. Reducing steps and decision-making load mattered more than adding features. In the next iteration, I want to improve recipe clarity, add images to help users choose faster, and explore voice-first interaction more deeply.

Project 3 ‚Äî EduGlobe VR Campus Exploration

Description & Goal

Problem & Vision: Many students feel stressed when choosing where to study abroad. Websites and videos give information, but it's hard to imagine what everyday campus life actually feels like.

EduGlobe is a VR system that lets students walk around campuses worldwide. They can explore dorms, libraries, and canteens to understand the environment and feel before committing to a school.

  • Users: Students considering exchange or full degree programs abroad
  • Main task: Explore campuses by walking through VR simulations
  • Platform: Meta Quest headset + controllers (mocked up)

Part 1 ‚Äî Empathize

Interviews & Observations

I conducted interviews with HKUST students planning exchange or study abroad programs. I asked how they research schools and what feels unclear before making a decision.

Most students use rankings, websites, social media, and vlogs, but these feel "staged" and don't show real student life. Students worry about dorm quality, campus layout, food options, and social atmosphere.

Key Pain Points

  • Hard to picture daily life: Don't know what a typical day looks like on each campus.
  • Too many choices: Long partner school lists make it hard to compare fairly.
  • Fear of wrong choice: Afraid of choosing a school that doesn't fit their lifestyle.
  • Information overload: Lots of scattered online info that doesn't give a real experience.

Part 2 ‚Äî Ideate

From Insights to POV

Our team created a mind map of interview findings: emotional needs, research activities, and missing experiences (like walking around campus).

POV: A student comparing universities for exchange needs a way to really experience campus life because online information doesn't show how it actually feels to live and study there.

  • Mind map (overview)

Brainstormed Ideas

We explored several directions: School Spirit VR (sports and traditions), Academic Fit VR (lectures and labs), Culture Immersion VR (city exploration), and Campus Exploration VR (walking through key locations).

Speed-Dating with Storyboards

We ran "speed-dating" sessions showing different VR storyboards. Campus Exploration VR was consistently the most useful. Students liked jumping between dorms, library, and cafeteria to narrow down options.

  • Campus Exploration VR Storyboard

Part 3 ‚Äî Prototype

Core Experience

EduGlobe is a VR tour where users select a university, load a campus simulation, and teleport to major locations (dorms, library, cafeteria) using buttons. An "End Tour" option lets them exit and explore another school.

My Prototype Contribution: Web-Based Mockups

I designed and coded phone-sized web mockups showing three screens: campus selection, tour view with location buttons, and a final thank-you screen. These let our team share the concept online and demonstrate interactions without a full VR setup, making it easy for presentations and user feedback.

Equipment & Interaction

The full system uses Meta Quest headset and controllers. Users look at things and click to move and select locations. Smooth camera movement with text labels guides them. New campuses can be added as separate "worlds" with the same interaction patterns. We kept it simple so first-time VR users wouldn't feel overwhelmed.

Part 4 ‚Äî Evaluation

How Users Reacted

We showed our concept to students considering exchange. They liked seeing actual spaces instead of static photos, jumping between locations matched their daily routine, and appreciated an easy, cheap way to visit all university options.

Use of AI

Generative AI was used to generate ideas used in the storyboards. The web mockups I designed and coded were created independently to demonstrate the concept.

Personal Contributions & Achievements

  • Conducted student interviews about exchange decision-making and organized findings into clear problems and user needs.
  • Helped film the demo video to showcase the EduGlobe concept for easy online sharing.
  • Created prototype storytelling materials for presentations.
  • Identified key emotional and practical concerns students face when choosing study abroad programs.

Personal Reflection

This project showed me the importance of understanding users' real experiences, not just what they say they want. During interviews, students struggled to explain their needs, but their stories revealed real problems. For example, feeling "lost" while researching highlighted the need for intuitive exploration.

Knowledge gained: Good design comes from listening to what users don't directly say. I learned to pay attention to emotions and frustrations, connecting them to design features that solve problems.

Lessons learned: Creating the demo video was more valuable than expected. It forced me to explain the concept clearly and showed that testing ideas early by showing them to people works better than building everything first. The video helped us get feedback faster.

Next steps: Create an interactive prototype for navigating campus locations, then test with students actively choosing schools to see if it reduces stress and improves decision-making.
